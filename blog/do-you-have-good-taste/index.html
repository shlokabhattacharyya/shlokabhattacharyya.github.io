do you have good taste?

culture may 16, 2026 · 5 min read · link

artificial intelligence (AI) is everywhere, whether you're a fan of it or not. it's slowly seeping into the code behind the apps you use everyday, the writing in the book you've been seeing everywhere, the script of your favorite actor's role in their new movie, the design process of that new shirt you wanted — everywhere.

now, on one hand, this isn't new. all of these industries have always, to an extent, harbored some sorts of technologies that have helped make their content or productions “better” than if it was done purely by a human. but at what point must we stop and ask ourselves what we define as better?

the rise of generative AI has drastically changed what access to this technology looks like and is therefore significantly altering the quality of the content coming out of it. services that were previously locked away behind paywalls or credential titles are now available to the public. consider large language models (LLMs) for example. LLMS were initially trained on vast datasets spanning everything from critically acclaimed novels to credible journalistic publications to high-level scientific research papers; in other words, some of the best-quality human produced work. the original mission statement of openai was to “advance digital intelligence in the way that is most likely to benefit humanity as a whole, unconstrained by a need to generate financial return.” technologies like this were originally developed with the goal of taking the best of what we have and building off of it to continue furthering society for the better — and in some ways it has worked: medical developments, software advancements, scientific and mathematical discoveries, sustainability optimizations, financial efficiencies, etc.

but the problem with developing a product with a scope so wide is that you cannot always imagine what will come of it. when chatgpt launched less than four years ago, none of us could have conceptualized italian brainrot, kirkifying memes, or fruit love island. on the surface, they might seem funny and even meant to poke fun at the irony of putting in such little effort into creating something. but when we allow videos with such poor quality content to be what is going viral and taking over our feeds, that is when we must question if we find something funny because we enjoy making fun of it or because we truly find it entertaining.

with AI becoming more widespread, there is no doubt that on the surface we can produce seemingly better content. it can be an excellent proofreader, a wonderful prototyper, and a structured research agent. as AI continues to develop and we learn how to use it better, it no longer takes a years of education or professional training to create work that we deem better. so, as the barriers to access start to dissolve, taste becomes the differentiating factor.

having taste requires you to take a step above being drawn to certain definitions or aesthetics. it is no longer enough to decide what is “good”, you must also have the ability to discern between human-made and machine-made. human-made is not necessarily always good and machine-made is not necessarily always bad, but we must scrutinize the source and the idea. the issue is not inherently in the fact that people are creating with these tools, we should use tools to aid us in our creativity — the issue is when we let the tools replace us. technology can strip away the obstacles and allow anyone to make anything, but we must also be able to recognize what is worth making.

the true value of creativity has always been the fact that draws so intimately upon the human experience. our favorite books, our favorite poems, our favorite movies, our favorite songs. more than anything, we love how they make us feel. no amount of shortcuts or technology will ever be able to replicate those feelings to the same degree. this goes beyond just our consumption of the arts. my favorite academic papers are the ones that explain concepts deeply and concisely as if they're talking directly to you. my favorite websites to use are the ones with thoughtful user design and secret easter eggs. in a world where technology makes it so easy for us to take in so much all the time, it is up to you to decide what you are willing to consume. our attention is precious and should be weaponized by only letting ourselves spare it on only the best of the best.

our taste is also a direct reflection of how much we value the creative process. if we are willing to enjoy low-effort slop, we will become satisfied with creating low-effort slop ourselves. the human experience is tied so closely to building and making, but the importance of that comes inherently from our desire to expend our own energy to produce something. the creative process means nothing if you replaced it with a prompt that took you 20 seconds to type. it is disrespectful to yourself and anyone who's ever made anything before (so, everyone) to be so naïve to think that it could even come close.

on top of how it may have been entertaining or you may have learned something, a meaningful reason to enjoy content is the respect you have for the people who made it for undertaking the journey to go out there and seek to put their hard work and effort into something that is meant to be enjoyed. a romcom with dialogues written by LLMs cannot be felt as deeply because LLMs does not know how heartbreak pierces you in your stomach at 3am. a probability lecture from an AI-generated website cannot teach as effectively because an AI-generated website has never known what it is like to be confused over your math homework. a trendy shirt designed entirely by a foundational design model cannot be worn as freely because a foundational design model cannot perceive what is beautiful to the human eye. so if you, as a human, are willing to sacrifice your standards for feeling, learning, and branding because it is easy, you have poor taste. simple.

broadly speaking, lack of effort and good taste cannot coexist, and as improved technology removes the barrier of effort in creating and there becomes an abundance in what we could consume, we must all individually decide what we deem valuable and necessary to consume. taste is the new barrier, one that you must work hard to learn and develop over time, without shortcuts or technology telling you how to do it.
